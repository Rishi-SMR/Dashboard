# 05 — Workforce Security Awareness Training

**System:** SMR Cashflow Dashboard · **Effective:** 2026-07-18
**Authority:** §164.308(a)(5) (security awareness and training),
§164.530(b) (privacy training)

Training is required **before first access**, and again **annually** and after
any material change. The signed register in §7 is what proves it happened — the
session itself is not evidence.

Deliver this in about 20 minutes. It is deliberately specific to this dashboard
rather than generic HIPAA slides, because generic training does not change
behaviour.

---

## 1. Why this system is in scope

Sports Med Recovery is a healthcare provider, so patient information is
protected by federal law. This dashboard reads SMR's Striven data to show
revenue, receivables, orders and vendor activity.

You will not see patient names anywhere in it. You will see references like
`PT-418`. **That reference plus an order and an amount is still protected
information**, because SMR can look up who 418 is. Handle every screen and every
export as patient data.

Penalties are real: civil fines run from roughly $100 to over $60,000 per
violation, and knowing misuse can be criminal. More practically — a breach means
notifying patients, notifying the government, and possibly the press.

## 2. Your account

- **Your login is yours alone.** Never share it, never log in for someone else,
  never let a colleague "just use yours quickly." The system records every action
  against your name — someone else's mistake becomes your record.
- **Use a unique password**, at least 12 characters, not reused from email,
  banking, or any other site.
- **Five wrong attempts locks the account for 15 minutes.** If that happens
  unexpectedly, tell the Security Officer — it may mean someone is guessing.
- Your session lasts 12 hours. **Lock your screen** whenever you step away
  (`Ctrl/⌘ + L`). An unlocked screen is an open door.
- If you leave SMR or change roles, your access is removed the same day.

## 3. The rules that matter day to day

**Do:**
- Log in only from an SMR work device with disk encryption and a screen lock
- Refer to patients by their `PT-` reference in emails, tickets and chat
- Delete CSV exports as soon as you are finished with them
- Report anything odd the same day — you will never be criticised for reporting

**Do not:**
- Log in from a public, shared, or hotel computer
- Email, message, or upload an export outside SMR
- Paste dashboard data into ChatGPT, Claude, a translator, or any online tool
  SMR has not approved — that is a disclosure to a third party
- Take screenshots for personal reference or store them on a personal phone
- Look up a patient, a friend, or a colleague out of curiosity. **Every read is
  logged with your name, the page, the time and your IP.** Curiosity browsing is
  one of the most commonly enforced HIPAA violations.
- Print reports unless you have a business need — and shred them afterwards

## 4. Phishing — the most likely way this goes wrong

The lock-out and the hashing protect against guessing. Neither protects against
you typing your password into a convincing fake page.

Be suspicious of: urgency ("your access expires today"), a login page reached
from a link rather than your bookmark, a sender address that is nearly-but-not
your domain, unexpected attachments, or anyone phoning to ask for a code or
password. **SMR will never ask for your password by email, chat, or phone.**

If you clicked something and then had second thoughts: say so immediately. Fast
reporting turns a breach into a near-miss.

## 5. Minimum necessary

Access only what your job requires, and no more. If a report shows you more than
you need, that is worth mentioning — the system is designed to send the browser
the least data that answers the question, and we would rather tighten it.

## 6. Reporting

Tell the Security Officer the same day if you see: a name where a `PT-` code
should be, an export sent somewhere it should not have gone, a lost or stolen
device, a login you do not recognise, a shared password, or anything that simply
looks wrong.

There is no penalty for reporting in good faith. There is a real one for
staying quiet.

## 8. Quick check (five questions — ask them verbally)

1. A colleague's account is locked and they ask to use yours for five minutes.
   What do you do? → *Refuse; contact the Security Officer. Sharing is prohibited.*
2. You export a vendor report and email it to your personal address to finish at
   home. Is that OK? → *No — an unapproved disclosure. Use an SMR device.*
3. `PT-418` is not a patient name, so the data is anonymous. True? → *False.
   SMR holds the key, so it is still protected information.*
4. You get an email saying your dashboard access expires in one hour, click here.
   → *Do not click. Go to the bookmark directly. Report it.*
5. You accidentally see a figure you had no reason to look at. Do you report it?
   → *Yes — same day. Good-faith reports are never penalised.*
